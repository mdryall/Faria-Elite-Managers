Subject: Our elite status paper — a full draft, at last

Dear João,

Our market-for-elite-status project has sat fallow for too long, and I owe you an apology for the silence and, I hope, some compensation for it: attached is a complete draft, 43 pages, with the two halves of our old project finally welded into one economy.

Let me give you the short version of what the paper now is. We price elite status as a club membership in the general equilibrium framework of Ellickson, Grodal, Scotchmer, and Zame, which turns out to be exactly the right machine for our question. The static model delivers three results I think you will like. First, equilibrium compensation reproduces what the data show: elite managers earn visible salary premia yet accept lower payoffs net of their elite education, and the premium decomposes exactly into excess tuition minus the status discount. A sharp prediction falls out of the accounting: changes in elite tuition have zero incidence on elite salaries, because the status discount absorbs them one for one. Second, aggregate output falls as the taste for status spreads, even though elite firms out-produce their rivals firm by firm; what matters is output per unit of resources, and the elite school system burns resources that no firm's accounts ever show. Third, and centrally, every such equilibrium lies in the core: no entrant, coalition, or policy commanding lump-sum instruments can displace the elite sector. The market is not failing to correct elite waste; there is nothing, in the welfare sense, to correct. Markets work; material efficiency does not follow. The welfare theorem breaks only when status is valued for its scarcity, in which case each entrant dilutes every incumbent, there is genuine over-entry, and a membership tax can raise welfare and output together.

The dynamics are where your original conception lives, and I want to be direct about how it evolved. The overlapping-generations frame is yours; the within-period allocation is now the club equilibrium itself, with per-capita wealth as the state variable, and your savings margin appears as the transfer share in a warm-glow specification. One condition, the elite sector's resource-productivity deficit, simultaneously governs the static output decline, makes status a luxury (richer economies endogenously support larger elites), and makes the elite sector a drag on accumulation: growth finances the status spending that arrests it, and the economy converges, with the path fully foreseen by everyone on it, to a steady state permanently poorer than its status-free counterfactual. In working this out I found that our old two-period formulation hits a structural snag (financing old-age consumption requires the old to own the capital stock, which unravels the wage anchor), so the draft handles the transfer motive differently and proves the mechanism is invariant to it; an appendix documents all of this carefully.

To be clear about where things stand: this is a working draft, not a finished product. Every derivation is verified in companion code and the draft has been through several rounds of internal review, but I intend a full editing pass after you have had your say, and there are open questions where your macro instincts are precisely what the paper needs, above all dynasty-specific inheritance with forward-looking parents, and the intertemporal welfare question, both flagged in the discussion section.

On placement, my proposal is JPE first: the closest ancestors (Becker-Murphy-Werning, Cole-Mailath-Postlewaite, Fershtman-Murphy-Weiss) are all JPE papers, and the thesis is general-interest in the way that journal rewards. ReStud would be my second choice. I do not see this as an Econometrica paper given its focus. Tell me what you think.

Read at whatever pace suits you and send comments in whatever form is easiest. If you would like access to the project repository, with the code, the full review record, and the working notes, I will set you up; but the PDF is self-contained.

It is good to have this moving again, and I think you will find the destination worth the wait.

Best,
Mike
