## Findings

**1. FATAL (internal contradiction in a headline "testable implication").** [FIXED — now the lead prediction, reversed]
"Gross elite premia should co-move with elite tuition, because the premium *is* tuition reimbursement" is **false in the model**. The gross elite wage is −q₄.₁ = w_E − k + τr with w_E = z, and z contains −τr, so −q₄.₁ = α[c+r+(μ+2λ−3)k] − βc + (3−2λᵉ)k − k — **independent of τ**. A rise in elite tuition is absorbed one-for-one by the status discount; pass-through to gross pay is exactly zero (verified at the paper's own parameters: gross wage 7.75 at τ=4 and still 7.75 at τ=2). Co-movement emerges only under variation in r, at rate d(premium)/dr = α−1. The model actually predicts *zero* incidence of elite tuition on gross pay — itself an interesting and different testable claim. [The repair adopts exactly this: zero pass-through is now the headline discriminating prediction, machine-verified.]

**2. MAJOR — Dale–Krueger (2002) characterized selectively.** [FIXED]
"Near-zero average selectivity premia" omits: (a) significantly positive payoffs for low-income/disadvantaged students; (b) DK's finding that the average tuition/cost of the school attended predicts later earnings — contrary to the manuscript's reading. The authors' own Chetty–Deming–Friedman citation explicitly reinterprets DK (matriculation designs miss upper-tail effects). Citing both in one sentence without reconciliation was internally inconsistent.

**3. MAJOR — The bound "gross premium ≤ excess tuition" is quantitatively falsified by the papers cited.** [FIXED: reframed as bounding the status component]
CDF find Ivy-Plus attendance raises mean earnings by ~$101,000 at age 33 (against a counterfactual mean of ~$143,000), top-1% odds ~50–60%, and roughly triples prestigious-firm employment; Zimmerman finds +44% leadership and +51% top-0.1% odds in Chile with modest tuition differentials; net-of-aid Ivy tuition is often below flagship tuition, so excess tuition can be negative while the premium is large. The decomposition therefore cannot cap total premia; the repair states that it bounds the status-financed component, with scarcity rents and bundled ability carrying the remainder.

**4. MAJOR — "Net returns at or below the non-elite track" conflicted with known evidence and was misattributed to DK.** [FIXED]
No existing estimate finds negative net returns to elite tracks; the claim is now labeled as the model's untested prediction, with the contrary causal evidence acknowledged and routed through scarcity rents. "The precise configuration observed" corrected — the net position is a prediction, not an observation.

**5. MAJOR — "Tuition reimbursement, not productivity" not identified against standard alternatives.** [FIXED]
Competitive human-capital models also predict wages reimburse education costs. The distinguishing implications are the (untested) negative net return and the zero tuition pass-through — the latter now stated as the discriminating experiment (tuition-shock episodes in full-cost credential markets).

**6. MAJOR — The firm-accounts vs national-accounts wedge was not well-posed against actual measurement.** [FIXED]
NIPA measures education output at input cost, so the burn is booked as education-sector value added, not a fall in measured GDP; "declining aggregate TFP" does not follow without an explicit mapping. Magnitudes: elite institutions are far too small a share of GDP to move aggregate TFP trends, and the largest elite-credentialing economies are TFP leaders. The salvageable, measurable analog is the resource cost per elite position (the credential-inflation margin) — now the stated observable, with an honest magnitude note.

**7. MAJOR — "Markets for expensive, skill-free credentials should be absent in poor economies" has salient historical counterexamples.** [FIXED]
Venal offices in ancien régime France, purchased British commissions, Qing sale of examination degrees, traded titles; intense credentialism in modern poor countries. Diagnosable source: uniform endowment k equates "poor economy" with "everyone poor." Restated as a within-economy comparative static holding the distribution fixed, with the endowment-inequality caveat and the historical examples now cited in-text.

## Overall assessment (as delivered, pre-repair)

The theory is elegant and the headline citations are individually accurate at abstract level, but the manuscript's empirical interface was substantially overclaimed and in one place contradicted the model's own mathematics. None of this damages the core theorems, which are about possibility and welfare, not magnitudes; the fix is to rewrite the empirical interface around explicitly derived comparative statics, honest magnitude comparisons, and a clear statement of what would distinguish the status-discount mechanism from human-capital and rent-sharing alternatives. As drafted pre-repair, a JPE/AER referee would have recommended rejection on the empirical framing while acknowledging the theoretical contribution.
