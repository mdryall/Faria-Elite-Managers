## Executive assessment

### Overall classification for JPE: **MAJOR REVISION**

This is no longer a rejection-level project. The revision has substantially answered the most important conceptual objection in the earlier strategic assessment. In particular, the new membership-equivalence definition and displacement theorem now state the paper's central message in general-equilibrium language:

> Competition disciplines institutions in the space of the complete products they sell. Material output is only a projection of that product when institutional membership, qualification, or standing is itself valued.

That is an economically important observation, especially in current debates in which the survival of materially costly elite institutions is treated as prima facie evidence of market power, capture, informational failure, or blocked entry. The paper now shows precisely why that inference need not follow. If a rival supplies the same qualification, the same status, and the same role feasibility at lower resource cost, the incumbent cannot survive. A materially cheaper rival that does not supply the same membership product need not displace it. The revision therefore does answer the partial-equilibrium objection rather than assuming it away.

The paper also has several unusually compelling complementary findings:

- the linked school–firm chain can look productive on firm accounts while reducing material output on system accounts;
- the gross elite wage premium can coexist with a lower net material position once education costs are included;
- the premium decomposes into excess tuition less the status discount;
- the members of a status-producing group directly finance its material shortfall through membership prices;
- costly absolute status can be Pareto-efficient consumption, while positional status creates a class-wide dilution externality and over-entry;
- correct anticipation need not eliminate the within-period status margin in the specified dynamic class.

These are serious contributions. They make the Ellickson–Grodal–Scotchmer–Zame framework economically useful rather than merely ornamental.

The manuscript is nevertheless not ready to submit to JPE in its present form. The remaining problems are not a hidden equilibrium contradiction. They are:

1. omission of very close recent literature;
2. reliance on a binary, primitive qualification restriction at the exact point that sustains coexistence;
3. presentation of a sufficient single-type displacement result as if it were a complete boundary for entire institutional systems;
4. a mismatch between the Turchin motivation and the object actually modeled;
5. residual forward-looking rhetoric stronger than the dynamic result;
6. an invalid stock/flow bridge in the new magnitude calculation;
7. a tuition experiment that does not yet correspond to the model's real-cost shock;
8. several small but real formal wording problems; and
9. a non-reproducible submission package as supplied.

The high-return route is bounded. The paper does **not** need a new foundational model, an endogenous theory of prestige, or a full quantitative macroeconomy. It needs one economically graded robustness result, sharper novelty positioning, and a disciplined claims-and-empirics revision.

## What changed relative to the earlier reviews

The earlier technical stress tests were mostly successful repair exercises. They found and then verified corrections to equilibrium characterization, the positional comparative statics, the inefficiency and tax arguments, transition-map scope, stability language, the empirical wage-pass-through prediction, and numerous notation and exposition problems. Those repairs remain valuable and, on a fresh read, the central algebra and equilibrium structure continue to look coherent.

The later strategic assessment asked a different question: even if the model is correct, does the paper make a sufficiently clear and important contribution beyond applying inherited machinery? The current revision directly targets that question. Its main additions—membership equivalence, the displacement theorem, the pricing proof, the open-set coexistence result, the financing corollary, the transfer-motive appendix, and the magnitude calculation—are therefore assessed here as substantive additions, not presumed valid because the revision memorandum says they are.

## Response to the authors' substantive defense

The authors' defense of the paper's central message is persuasive.

The usual claim that more efficient firms will displace less efficient firms is indeed a partial-equilibrium claim when “efficiency” is measured only in material output while the transacted bundle includes status, qualification, access, or command. A market does not generally eliminate a costly product merely because an observer deletes one valued characteristic before comparing it with another product. The correct comparison is between complete products.

The new theorem makes that statement testable inside the model rather than rhetorical. The serious school is cheaper and supplies the same school amenity, but it does not confer the qualification needed for the elite-management seat. It is consequently not a supplier of the same complete career product. Conversely, a replacement preserving feasibility and utility while using fewer goods cannot coexist with the incumbent. That is the right general-equilibrium answer to the displacement objection.

The authors are also correct that the dynamic extension belongs in the paper. Once static competitive coexistence is established, the immediate objection is whether forward-looking agents would anticipate the accumulation loss and undo the mechanism. The paper should answer that objection. The present extension shows something meaningful: under a common degree-one intraperiod aggregator and a common multiplicative continuation or savings factor, anticipation can change saving and the wealth path without changing the occupational/status margin at a given wealth level.

The needed revision is therefore not “delete the dynamics.” It is “state exactly what the dynamics establish.” The model does not yet prove that unrestricted altruistic dynasties knowingly select a lower-wealth social path, nor does it establish an intertemporal Pareto comparison. The right claim is that correct anticipation **need not** dissolve the status market, not that a collective rational society has been shown to choose impoverishment under general forward-looking preferences.

## General-equilibrium assessment

### What now works

#### 1. Membership equivalence is the correct economic object

Conditions (E1) and (E2) do important separate work:

- (E1) preserves the feasibility of complete lists, including prerequisites and qualifications;
- (E2) preserves utility wherever the original and substituted lists are feasible.

This is an effective way to formalize the fact that a school amenity and a qualification are distinct dimensions of the product. It also prevents a false proof based on swapping one seat while ignoring its consequences for the rest of an agent's list.

#### 2. The core proof is sound

For a same-profile replacement that preserves list feasibility and utility and has a strictly larger net-output vector, the grand coalition can replace all incumbent groups, preserve consistency, and distribute the released resources. With a positive measure of agents consuming interior bundles, the distribution can make a positive measure strictly better off and everyone else weakly better off. This is a valid strong-core block.

The proof is economically transparent: an institution that supplies exactly the same complete membership product while releasing real resources is not merely “less attractive at some prices”; it is coalitionally dominated.

#### 3. The pricing proof is especially valuable

The second proof is more important for the paper's competitive interpretation. Strictly positive commodity prices give positive value to the resource saving. Subtracting group budget-balance equations orders the aggregate price of the two seat bundles. If the incumbent is active, aggregate list substitution generates a strictly positive saving; a positive measure of agents can therefore make affordable, strictly preferred substitutions.

This route avoids relying on the inherited welfare theorem and applies at a fixed positional-status mass because the substitution preserves the set of elite-characteristic holders. It is the strongest response to a referee who might otherwise dismiss the displacement result as only a cooperative-core statement.

#### 4. The open-set result is analytically credible

At the numerical example, the relevant equilibrium and interiority inequalities are strict. The closed-form equilibrium objects vary continuously with the continuous parameters. Hence the active-elite equilibrium persists on an open neighborhood. This is enough analytically; the reported grids and random perturbations are supporting diagnostics, not the proof of openness.

The baseline numerical values reported for the new theorem reproduce:

- \(p_2=2\);
- \(z=5.75\);
- \(\eta^\ast\approx0.391304\);
- \(m^\ast\approx0.01998\);
- \(n\approx0.11396\);
- \(\sigma_0\approx0.59819\).

The serious school strictly saves material input, matches the school amenity, and fails (E1) at the elite-manager career list. Thus coexistence is not a numerical accident.

#### 5. The paper is now honest about the technology set

The discussion beginning “First, the feasible set takes the group-type list \(G\) as given” is a significant improvement. Every production model conditions on feasible technologies. The paper need not endogenize the invention of a reputation technology to establish a conditional equilibrium theorem. It must only avoid presenting credibility as if it were generated endogenously when it is imposed through consumption-set feasibility.

### Formal repairs still required

These are limited repairs, but a stringent GE theorist will notice them.

#### A. Put the replacement type in the economy

Theorem `thm:displacement(i)` should explicitly state either:

\[
g,g'\in G,
\]

or that the theorem concerns an augmented economy \(G^+=G\cup\{g'\}\).

Both proofs use consistency and budget balance for \(g'\). Those objects exist only when \(g'\) is a feasible type in the economy being analyzed. The prose later refers to the “augmented economy,” but the theorem statement should not leave this implicit.

#### B. Correct two literally false or self-contradictory sentences

In part (ii), the serious school cannot dominate “in precisely the sense of (i)” while failing (E1), because membership equivalence in part (i) includes (E1). Say instead that it strictly materially dominates and satisfies (E2), but fails (E1).

The sentence “No type in \(G\) is membership-equivalent to either elite type” is literally false because every type is membership-equivalent to itself. It should say “No **other** type in \(G\) …”

#### C. Do not call a sufficient condition a complete characterization

The theorem proves:

- membership equivalence plus strict material dominance is sufficient for displacement; and
- material dominance alone is not sufficient.

It does not prove an if-and-only-if characterization of every way an incumbent institution or a multi-institution chain can be displaced. The phrases “draws the boundary,” “in the following exact sense,” and “concedes the strongest form” are defensible only if immediately qualified as this sufficient/counterexample boundary.

Most importantly, the theorem replaces one group type with another type sharing its profile. It does not prove that a rival school type necessarily “displaces the elite system.” An elite firm could remain while the dominated school type disappears. Unless the authors generalize the theorem to a mapping between collections of types or complete institutional lists, “system” should become “incumbent institution” or “group type.”

#### D. Close the EGSZ boundary argument rather than asserting it

The appendix acknowledges that Cobb–Douglas utility is not strictly monotone on the axes, while the cited EGSZ theorem maintains strict monotonicity. The paper says the invocation is “unaffected” because local nonsatiation and budget exhaustion suffice. That is plausible, but it is a claim about the proof of an inherited theorem, not a demonstrated result.

The clean repair is a short self-contained welfare/core lemma for this economy:

1. individual optimization plus group budget balance imply no feasible coalition can make all members weakly and some strictly better off at the supporting prices;
2. desirable endowments provide the strengthening needed for the strong-core conclusion.

Alternatively, cite and verify an exact version of the EGSZ result under local nonsatiation. The current one-sentence assertion is beneath the manuscript's otherwise high formal standard.

#### E. Qualify the financing corollary

The identity

\[
\sum_\omega \pi(\omega)q(\omega,g)=-p\cdot y_g
\]

is correct and useful. It shows that a group's material shortfall is financed **directly on the group's accounts** by the aggregate prices of its seats. It does not, by itself, establish ultimate economic or welfare incidence, because a member can finance a payment from earnings obtained through other groups in her complete list.

The four-type compensation decomposition and occupational indifference do provide the more specific incidence result. The general corollary should distinguish direct accounting incidence from ultimate welfare incidence. Its value is interpretation and generality, not independent formal depth; the paper is right to acknowledge that it is group budget balance rearranged.

### Agent capabilities and interpretation

Agents choose complete school–occupation lists at posted prices with full knowledge of the equilibrium environment. Qualifications and organizational characteristics are observable and contractible. Group types can be formed at arbitrary mass. There is no admission risk, unsuccessful career attempt, or uncertainty over placement.

These are strong capabilities, but they are not incoherent in an Arrow–Debreu/EGSZ benchmark. The most effective interpretation is:

> Agents trade complete contingent career bundles in a maximal-competition benchmark. Persistence therefore does not rely on search frictions, mistakes, uncertainty, or a failure to anticipate prices.

That interpretation strengthens the possibility result. The manuscript should not describe this literally as students and firms simultaneously signing a real-world complete career contract. It is a decentralization device for an institutional allocation.

### Is the clubs apparatus essential?

A GE referee may note that the school groups are singleton groups and that baseline status utility attaches to an elite managerial characteristic rather than directly to peer composition. Some equations could be reproduced in a simpler hedonic career-bundle model.

The defense should be concrete. The EGSZ structure adds:

- consistency across linked school and firm memberships;
- endogenous formation of organizations with complementary roles;
- separate seat prices that jointly determine tuition and wages;
- nonconvex list and qualification constraints;
- group-level zero-profit accounting;
- arbitrary coalitional reformation and strong-core discipline; and
- a precise contrast between within-group composition effects and an economy-wide positional externality.

The key point is not that no simpler model could reproduce a wage equation. It is that this framework asks whether the entire institutional allocation survives reformation by arbitrary coalitions, rather than only whether atomistic individuals optimize at fixed prices.

### GE classification

**ACCEPTABLE on substantive GE logic, subject to the limited formal repairs above.**

The earlier fixed-\(G\) objection has been substantially answered. Remaining GE risk concerns theorem scope and economic interpretation, not equilibrium incoherence.

## Macro and dynamics assessment

### Why the extension belongs

The dynamic section answers the right objection. Static coexistence naturally prompts: if agents understand that the status sector lowers future resources, why do they not avoid it? Embedding the period economy in an accumulation map is therefore not an unrelated attempt to make the paper “macro.” It is a robustness and propagation exercise tied directly to the main mechanism.

The luxury and drag results are conceptually useful:

- the fixed resource requirement makes the elite path affordable to more agents as wealth rises;
- elite expansion absorbs resources and lowers the next-period resource base relative to the elite-free counterfactual;
- falling wealth eventually limits elite participation, preventing collapse in the analyzed configuration.

The revision also now acknowledges that the luxury property comes from a technological nonhomotheticity—fixed input requirements against wages that scale with wealth—rather than being assumption-free.

### What Proposition `prop:invariance` actually establishes

The proposition assumes period utility of the form

\[
u=d(\ell;\eta)A(x_1,x_2,b)Q,
\]

where \(A\) is homogeneous of degree one and \(Q\) is common across agents. Under common expenditure shares, the common factor cancels from occupational comparisons. Membership prices, wages, the output price, the cutoff, and the elite mass at a given \(k\) are therefore invariant; the savings share changes the competent-sector scale and transition map.

This is a meaningful result. It shows that a broad class of common forward-looking or transfer motives need not change the within-period status allocation.

It is not a theorem about unrestricted forward-looking dynasties:

- a dynasty-specific continuation value need not be common across agents or occupations;
- elite and non-elite descendants may inherit different resources;
- the status choice may affect a child's state and hence fail to cancel from the occupational comparison;
- the paper does not establish intertemporal Pareto efficiency or show that a planner knowingly selects the lower-wealth path.

The Discussion admits this: replacing warm glow with forward-looking altruism would turn “rationality of decline” into a theorem about expectations, “though we have not proven this.” That sentence is honest, but it conflicts with the introduction's broader “Rational societies really do let it happen” and with “Nor does the conclusion rest on parents who fail to look ahead.”

The correct front-end statement is:

> Correct anticipation need not eliminate the status margin. Within the degree-one common-factor class, it changes saving and the wealth path but not status demand at a given wealth level.

This answer is sufficient for the paper's purpose. A full heterogeneous-dynasty model is not required before submission if the rhetoric is narrowed.

### The pooled-bequest result needs tighter interpretation

Proposition `prop:commons` has a sound underlying atomistic-pooling logic if parents strictly value their own current consumption, individual gifts have measure zero in the inheritance pool received by a child, and gifts are nonnegative. The phrase “purely altruistic” is not, however, a formal preference specification. If it meant that a parent cared only about the child and not about own consumption, the proof's strict cost-of-giving argument would not follow. The proposition should display an additive or multiplicative parental utility with strict monotonicity in own consumption and define the parent–child link under pooling.

More importantly, the result does not show that altruism strengthens the relative status drag. It implies zero transfers in both the elite and status-free economies under the stated pooling environment. The aggregate-gift commons overwhelms the accumulation mechanism rather than amplifying the elite-versus-no-elite wealth gap. The Discussion's suggestion that pooled altruism “plausibly strengthens the drag” should be deleted or recast.

### “No collapse” needs its return condition

The paper's formal transition proposition correctly makes repulsion from the origin depend on

\[
\theta(1-\zeta)\phi>c+r.
\]

The condition \(\Delta<0\) only implies that the elite sector disappears near the origin. It does not by itself make the elite-free economy accumulate away from zero. General prose such as “there is no collapse” and “status spending … does not extinguish” should therefore read:

> Provided the elite-free accumulation technology repels the origin, falling wealth shuts down the elite sector before status can generate a death spiral.

Without that return condition, convergence to zero can occur for reasons already present in the elite-free economy.

### The exact steady-state-loss identity

The algebraic identity in `rem:loss` is correct for the stated interior steady states. Let

\[
H=(\tau-1)r+(\beta-1)c
\]

denote physical burn per elite unit and

\[
D=(\beta c+\tau r)-\alpha(c+r)=|\Delta|
\]

denote the resource-productivity gap. Then the manuscript's multiplier can be rewritten as

\[
M=\frac{D/H}{1-\xi_n'(k_n^\ast)},
\]

because at the no-elite steady state

\[
\xi_n'(k_n^\ast)=
\frac{c+r}{\theta(1-\zeta)\phi}.
\]

This representation is economically clearer:

- \(D/H\) is the share of gross burn that survives after accounting for the elite firm's relative productivity;
- \(1/[1-\xi_n']\) is the familiar dynamic amplification associated with persistence in the no-elite transition.

It also reveals why the running example's \(M=1.25\) is not a general bound. The multiplier can become large as the no-elite convergence slope approaches one. A credible magnitude exercise must discipline or show sensitivity over both components, rather than importing the example's multiplier into U.S. data.

A small formal repair is also needed: the multiplier form divides by \(H\), so `rem:loss` should assume \(H>0\). The condition \(\Delta<0\) alone does not logically imply positive burn; for example, \(\tau=\beta=1\) and \(\alpha<1\) can give \(\Delta<0\) with \(H=0\). The maintained numerical application already has \(H>0\).

### Macro classification

**MAJOR REVISION if the paper asks the macro reader to validate a general forward-looking-impoverishment claim. ACCEPTABLE as a bounded propagation/robustness extension once the rhetoric and magnitude mapping are repaired.**

The dynamics should remain in the paper, but they should be subordinate to the institutional GE contribution. The paper is not, and need not become, a calibrated macro theory of aggregate stagnation.

## Empirical and magnitude assessment

### What has improved

The revised empirical section is much more disciplined than the earlier version.

- It correctly derives zero pass-through from the elite schooling-cost parameter \(\tau\) to the gross elite wage.
- It no longer claims the model's tuition decomposition caps all observed wage premia; it treats scarcity rents and bundled ability as possible additional components.
- It distinguishes firm accounts, resource accounts, and measured national accounts.
- It identifies credentialing resources per managerial position as a closer observable than aggregate TFP.
- It explicitly says the model is a mechanism exercise rather than a calibration and concedes that formal elite credentialing is too small to explain aggregate stagnation.

That concession helps rather than hurts the paper. The contribution can be important for institutional interpretation and incidence even if its aggregate U.S. wealth effect is small.

### The magnitude calculation is not yet a model-consistent bound

In the exact identity,

\[
b=\frac{m^\ast H}{k_e^\ast},
\]

so \(b\) is burn divided by the inherited resource stock used as the period input/endowment. The empirical paragraph replaces this with postsecondary expenditure divided by GDP. That is a different ratio:

- education expenditure is an annual flow measured in dollars;
- GDP is annual value added;
- \(k_e^\ast\) is the model's inherited resource base, repeatedly interpreted as wealth or productive input.

No equation currently maps the expenditure/GDP share into \(m^\ast H/k_e^\ast\). The subsequent \(M b\) calculation is therefore not a formal empirical bound.

There are three possible repairs:

1. derive the loss identity using burn as a share of model output and supply the equilibrium conversion factor;
2. estimate or calibrate the model's output-to-resource ratio and explicitly convert an empirical burn/output share into \(b\); or
3. label the calculation an illustrative envelope under an explicit normalization, not a bound implied by the model.

The first option is theoretically cleanest. The second is most ambitious. The third is acceptable if the paper's purpose is simply to show that plausible scale is modest.

There is also a numerator mismatch. The theoretical burn term

\[
H=(\tau-1)r+(\beta-1)c
\]

contains both excess schooling inputs and excess elite-firm inputs. The empirical numerator uses postsecondary expenditure only. Either call \(b\) the elite-complex burn share and measure or bound both components; split school and firm burn into separate terms; or set \(\beta=1\) for a credentialing-only empirical exercise and recompute the corresponding multiplier.

Nor is “burn” directly observable. Expenditure can be observed, but its division among skill creation, research, medical services, amenities, screening, and pure status production is an attribution. The paper's polar assumptions are legitimate for an extreme scenario, but the object should be called an attributed or accounting burn share, not an observable burn share.

The input data themselves are correctly quoted but broad. NCES reports \$702 billion in total 2020–21 postsecondary expenses, but that total includes research, public service, hospitals, housing, auxiliary enterprises, and other non-credentialing activities. The source itself emphasizes these categories. Consequently, using all spending as “burn” is extreme in the intended direction, but the paper should say explicitly that this is not a measure of credential production. The BEA source reports 2021 current-dollar GDP of \$23.00 trillion, so the roughly three-percent arithmetic is correct. See the [NCES expense definition](https://nces.ed.gov/programs/coe/indicator/cue/postsecondary-institution-expense) and [BEA's 2021 GDP release](https://www.bea.gov/index.php/news/2022/gross-domestic-product-third-estimate-corporate-profits-and-gdp-industry-fourth-quarter).

Finally, the assumed five-percent segment, four-times-average spending, and 100-percent burn attribution are deliberately extreme choices, but they are not demonstrated upper limits. The resulting 0.6 percent is therefore a conditional scenario or illustrative envelope, not a literal empirical bound.

The Ivy-Plus scale sentence also mixes denominators. “Fewer than half of one percent of Americans” is not comparable with the hypothetical segment's share of **students**. A published college-level table reports Ivy-Plus institutions as approximately 0.9 percent of college-goers, not below 0.5 percent of college-goers. The manuscript should use one denominator consistently and verify that the cited Chetty–Deming–Friedman source supports the chosen figure. See the [QJE college-tier table](https://academic.oup.com/qje/article/135/3/1567/5741707).

### The tuition experiment needs a real-cost shock

Within the model,

\[
q_2=\tau r
\]

is imposed by competitive school budget balance. The parameter \(\tau r\) is a real resource requirement. An observed change in posted tuition is not generally a change in that technology. It may reflect financial aid, price discrimination, markup, cross-subsidy, demand, or market power—none of which is in the baseline model.

Paying the full posted price does not solve this identification problem. The proposed test should instead be stated conditionally:

> Following an exogenous increase in the real resource cost of producing the elite credential that passes one-for-one into tuition in the competitive-school environment, the model predicts no corresponding change in gross elite salaries.

That remains a sharp implication, but the relevant shock is harder to find than a generic posted-tuition episode. If the authors want to use tuition-policy shocks, they need either a separate pricing wedge or a careful argument that the episode moved resource cost rather than only price.

### The “net return” literature claim should be narrower

The sentence saying that no current estimate supports or refutes the inferior complete net position is too broad. The safer and more accurate claim is:

> The causal studies reviewed here do not directly estimate the complete career payoff—gross earnings less the full education cost plus the value of status—defined by the model.

This says what the evidence does not identify without claiming an exhaustive literature search.

## Novelty and contribution

### The closest current literature is missing

This is the most immediate JPE submission risk.

Daniel Ferreira and Radoslawa Nikolowa's [“Prestige, Promotion, and Pay,” *Journal of Finance* 79(1), 2024](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jofi.13301), develops a theory in which professional-service firms design career structures to sell prestigious jobs to qualified candidates. It derives implications for compensation, employment, competition, and sector size. The overlap in verbal mechanism—firms selling prestige, qualified candidates, compensation, and the size of a prestige sector—is close enough that omission would concern an editor.

Manuel Staab's [“The Formation of Social Groups under Status Concern,” *Journal of Economic Theory* 222, 2024](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022053124001303), studies priced group formation with status concerns, peer quality, sorting, exclusion, and welfare under different market structures. It is directly relevant to the paper's claims about bringing status into a group-formation environment.

The compensation result must also be positioned against Rosen-style hedonic and equalizing-difference logic. Workers accepting lower material compensation for a desirable job characteristic is not by itself new.

These papers do not appear to subsume the manuscript. The distinctions are defensible:

- Ferreira–Nikolowa study designed internal career tournaments; this paper studies competitive group formation, a linked education-to-firm qualification chain, coalitional/core stability, resource incidence, and absolute versus positional welfare.
- Staab studies status and peer quality in social-group formation; this paper studies produced institutional status bundled with qualifications and control of material production.
- Hedonic theory gives the generic compensating-differential logic; this paper derives a linked tuition–wage identity and traces its implications for institutional survival and misleading firm-level accounts.

But the manuscript must make those distinctions explicitly. The current priority claim—“the first to use the framework to price status”—is too exposed until the literature is searched and delimited carefully.

### What is genuinely new

The new displacement theorem is correct and clarifying, but its first part is formally close to a dominance/no-arbitrage result: the same complete product with more ordinary goods blocks the incumbent. The financing corollary is group budget balance rearranged. These results should not be asked to carry the entire novelty burden.

The stronger contribution is the institutional combination:

> When status is bundled with a qualification and control of a producing organization, competitive prices can sustain an institution dominated in the material projection, identify who directly finances its resource cost, and make firm-level performance a misleading guide to system-level productivity.

The paper then adds a sharp welfare boundary:

- absolute status can be ordinary, efficient consumption;
- positional status creates an external effect spanning multiple groups;
- within-group membership prices do not internalize that class-wide dilution.

The linked school–firm implementation, compensation decomposition, core discipline, and accounting distinction are what elevate the paper above “EGSZ plus an example.”

### The qualification restriction is still doing decisive work

The serious-school graduate cannot occupy the elite managerial seat. Agents may attend at most one school. The complete list is chosen without admission or placement risk. Group types form at arbitrary mass, so every elite student in equilibrium can be matched to an elite managerial role.

This is coherent, and the revised paper now admits that credibility is primitive. But the open-set result varies continuous parameters while leaving a discrete zero-portability restriction fixed. It does not show that coexistence survives when serious credentials have even a small probability or degree of access to elite control.

The single highest-return formal addition is therefore a graded portability result. For example, introduce a portability or recognition parameter \(\rho\) that gives the alternative credential partial access to the elite role or partial status/qualification equivalence. Characterize a threshold:

- above a sufficiently strong degree of complete-product equivalence, the material advantage induces displacement;
- below it, willingness to pay for the differentiated qualification supports coexistence.

This need not endogenize reputation. It would convert the current binary boundary into an economically interpretable comparative static and directly answer “the result is encoded in \(G\).”

A smaller fallback is to permit elite-qualified agents to take competent-manager positions and show that the added cross-path is unused or does not disturb the equilibrium. That would reduce the impression that two completely segregated career tracks mechanically generate the result.

## Motivation and interpretation

### The Turchin hook does not match the modeled mechanism

The introduction defines “elite overproduction” as an expansion of elite aspirants beyond the positions available to them. The model does not contain that scarcity:

- elite schooling and elite-manager positions expand together;
- consistency gives each elite graduate a managerial seat;
- there are no disappointed aspirants, excluded graduates, or unemployed would-be elites;
- the supply of elite organizations is not fixed.

The model delivers **elite-sector expansion** and greater resource absorption by elite institutions. That is interesting, but it is not Turchin's narrow aspirant-to-position congestion mechanism.

The paper can retain Turchin as motivation if it says explicitly:

> We formalize the resource-absorption and elite-expansion component of this debate, not the excess-supply-of-aspirants mechanism.

Thereafter, propositions should use “elite expansion” or “proliferation of elite institutions.” Reserve “elite overproduction” for the broader motivating literature unless the paper defines its own narrower use.

Likewise, the motivating claim about managers selected for pedigree rather than productivity should not be presented as a modeled selection result. Agents have no productivity heterogeneity. The model compares institutional production technologies and qualification bundles, not the assignment of high- versus low-ability managers.

### Status and qualification remain primitives

The model locates why material competition need not displace the elite institution: the alternative credential does not confer the same qualification. It does not explain how that qualification becomes credible, why employers honor it, or how it decays.

This is an acceptable scope choice. The paper should sell itself as a theory of the consequences of priced institutional status under a given recognition technology, not as a theory of the origin of prestige or gatekeeping.

### Tier 0: Repair the submission package

The source-level labels and references do not show obvious duplicate-label or undefined-cross-reference problems, but a clean compile and the “machine-verified” claims cannot be independently checked from this package.

### Tier 1: Required no-new-model revision

These changes should precede JPE submission even if no new theorem is added.

1. Add and distinguish Ferreira–Nikolowa (2024), Staab (2024), and hedonic/equalizing-difference work.
2. Recast the novelty around the linked institutional chain, compensation incidence, core stability, and firm-versus-system accounting.
3. Fix the formal displacement wording: feasible \(g'\), “no other type,” and no “precisely the sense of (i)” contradiction.
4. Narrow “system” claims to the institution/type actually covered, unless the theorem is generalized.
5. Supply a self-contained core/welfare argument or an exact local-nonsatiation citation.
6. Correct the Turchin framing from excess aspirants to elite-sector expansion.
7. Replace “payoff” with “material/pecuniary position” where utility is not meant.
8. Narrow rationality, policy, and “every equilibrium” rhetoric to the actual maintained environment.
9. Repair the magnitude denominator and present sensitivity in \(D/H\) and \(1/(1-\xi_n')\).
10. Recast the tuition test around exogenous real schooling-cost shocks.
11. Use a consistent student-based denominator for Ivy-Plus scale and verify the supporting citation.

13. Formalize the pooled-bequest preference and carry the origin-repulsion condition into “no collapse” claims.

### Tier 2: One high-return formal addition

Add imperfect qualification portability or partial complete-product substitutability. This is the best use of additional theorem-building effort because it:

- attacks the strongest remaining “encoded in \(G\)” objection;
- gives economic content to the displacement boundary;
- makes coexistence robust to a less stark institutional segmentation;
- offers a natural comparative static that readers can map to recognition, accreditation, or gatekeeping;
- preserves the paper's core architecture.

The paper does **not** need to solve a full reputation-formation game. A threshold proposition is enough.

### Tier 3: Reorganize around the strongest contribution

The displacement theorem is described as “first, and organizing the rest,” but appears near the end of the equilibrium section. A cleaner architecture would:

1. state the general displacement result immediately after the framework;
2. characterize the four-type economy;
3. apply the theorem to explain why material dominance fails to displace the elite school;
4. present compensation and firm-versus-system accounting as the central implementation results;
5. present absolute versus positional welfare as the main extension;
6. retain dynamics as robustness and propagation rather than a coequal macro paper.

The abstract currently tries to carry displacement, compensation, output, Pareto efficiency, positional taxation, and dynamics. It should state one question, one mechanism, and at most three headline findings. The compensation decomposition and firm-versus-system accounting deserve more prominence than the financing corollary by itself.

### What not to do

The paper should not:

- abandon the market-for-status idea;
- delete the dynamic extension;
- build a full endogenous-reputation model solely to answer this review;
- claim quantitative macro importance unsupported by the mechanism's scale;
- present the displacement result as formally deep merely because it is now a theorem;
- add weak empirical exercises that do not map into model primitives.

## Recommended contribution statement

A defensible three-sentence version is:

> Competitive markets eliminate institutions dominated in the complete products they sell, but need not eliminate institutions dominated only in material output when membership and qualification are valued characteristics. In a linked school–firm club economy, an elite institutional chain can therefore survive in the strong core, be financed by its status-consuming members, and make productive-looking firms coexist with lower system-wide material output. Absolute status is efficient consumption in the baseline, whereas class-wide positional dilution creates over-entry; an accumulation extension shows that correct anticipation need not eliminate the underlying status margin.

This version makes the unusual application and its economic importance visible without claiming more than the model proves.

## Final recommendation

The revision has adequately dealt with most of the earlier structural objections. In particular:

- the partial-equilibrium critique is now formally answered;
- competitive entry is distinguished from invention of a status-equivalent technology;
- equilibrium and core justification are substantially sound;
- coexistence is not a knife-edge numerical artifact;
- the dynamic extension has a legitimate role;
- the paper now confronts, rather than evades, the likely small aggregate magnitude.

The remaining revision is material but not destructive. My recommendation is to keep JPE as the organizing target, complete the Tier 0 and Tier 1 repairs, and add the Tier 2 portability result before submission. With those changes, the paper would have a credible chance of being sent to referees and taken seriously as a top-journal theory contribution. Without them, the most likely failure is editorial: the close-literature omission, binary qualification technology, and visible empirical/claim mismatches may prevent the paper's strongest insight from receiving a full hearing.
